Origin

An ancient art, a modern bridge

Hong Kong's elderly are increasingly isolated by a digital divide that widens every year. As younger generations turn to technology for work and play, their grandparents are left behind — the gap not only technological, but emotional, cultural, a quiet erosion of connection.

Tai Chi — practised in parks at dawn, shared across generations — has always been a remedy for both body and mind. Research from Harvard Medical School and HKU links regular practice to slower cognitive decline, relief from depression, and stronger social bonds.

What if AI could make this ancient practice accessible to everyone — unconstrained by time, cost, or geography — and in doing so, bring generations together?

How It Works

AI that watches, learns, and guides

Maestro uses human pose estimation to observe your movements through a camera, compare them against expert Tai Chi demonstrations, and offer instant corrections — a private coach at the tap of a button.

01

Observe

OpenPose encodes your body as a skeleton of keypoints, adapting to any physique through Part Affinity Fields.

02

Compare

Your pose is compared against data from Tai Chi masters. AI catches misalignments human eyes might miss.

03

Correct

Incorrect joints glow red with audio cues. Correct ones are affirmed in green. Instant, personalised.

Core Technology

The science beneath the art

Maestro frames Tai Chi correction as two machine learning tasks: classifying which posture the user is attempting, and discriminating whether it is executed correctly. OpenPose's Part Affinity Fields encode the body independently of size, height, or shape — making feedback truly personalised.
